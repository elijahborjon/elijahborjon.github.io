About

A bit more about me.

I’m Elijah — a student researcher, musician, and person who has always lived in the space between worlds. I grew up translating for my family: medical updates, legal notices, school emails, anything that arrived in English but needed to be lived in Spanish. Those moments didn’t feel heroic; they felt like survival. But they also became the root of how I think about knowledge: if information can’t cross a barrier — linguistic, educational, or emotional — then it may as well not exist. That belief still shapes everything I do.

Curiosity is my default setting. I’m the kid who once pulled apart a fallen strand of beard moss just to figure out why a plant might need a tendon, freezing mid-trail with a damp white filament stretched between my fingers and a dozen new questions unfurling in my head :contentReference[oaicite:0]{index=0}. That same instinct shows up now in research, in music, and in the way strangers somehow end up telling me their life stories in grocery store lines. I think paying attention — to people, to ecosystems, to patterns — is its own form of care.

In my research, I follow the questions that tug at me, even when they look “meaningless” to everyone else — like why a yoga study might downregulate a receptor involved in chronic stress and cancer. Chasing those threads transformed my understanding of what it means to do science: not to be the smartest person in the room, but the one willing to stay with a hard question until it becomes clear enough for someone else to use :contentReference[oaicite:1]{index=1}.

Music is the other half of my education. Whether I’m at a piano, in a rehearsal room, or taking photos from the sidelines of a lacrosse field, I’m always looking for the small human universes inside a moment — the tired defender leaning on his long pole, the soft rustle of choir folders, the breath before a downbeat. Art, like science, is translation: turning attention into something that can be felt.

And if I’m honest, the person who teaches me the most about attention lately is my girlfriend, Nicoya. She’s the reminder that curiosity isn’t just an academic trait, but a relational one — a way of noticing someone fully, of learning the textures of their world, of choosing wonder on purpose.

This website is a small snapshot of all of that: the questions I’m asking, the work I’m building, the people and places that raised me, and the worlds I’m trying to translate with a little more care.
